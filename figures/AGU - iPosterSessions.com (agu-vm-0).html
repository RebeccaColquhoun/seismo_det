Spectral Scaling Transfer Function Method for Scenario Ground Motion Simulation with Application to the 2019 Ridgecrest Earthquake Sequence

Monica Kohler(1), Anthony Massari(2), Filippos Filippos(3), Thomas Heaton(1), Becky Roh(4)

1) California Institute of Technology; 2) Ohio State University; 3) Western Macedonia University; 4) Center for Naval Analyses

Abstract

A new spectral scaling transfer function approach is presented for simulating ground motions for scenario large-magnitude earthquakes when a smaller-magnitude earthquake from the same source region has been recorded. We use observed 2019 Ridgecrest California earthquake sequence ground motions to validate the transfer function method, including data from foreshocks with M>5. This method assumes that the distance to observation is large compared to the source dimensions, which is the case here with ground motions simulated for Los Angeles basin locations at a distance of 200 km from the Ridgecrest source region. We examine geographical variations in 3-second and 6-second pseudo-spectral accelerations for the observed M7.1 Ridgecrest earthquake, and for a scenario M7.6 Ridgecrest-like earthquake. We then apply the observed M7.1 and simulated M7.6 ground motions to linear dynamic analysis with finite-element models developed  for existing, instrumented 15-story and 52-story buildings. The results indicate that relative to the M7.1 earthquake, the M7.6 scenario earthquake will produce peak elastic inter-story drifts that are 2 times larger for the 15-story and 2.7 times larger for the 52-story. Given a set of observations made from the same source rupture mechanism in the same source region, this method is straightforward to implement as a transfer function to observed ground motions in the far field.

Spectral scaling transfer function method

     The derivation of the method is based on Aki’s theory of universal similarity of earthquake radiation (Aki, 1967), in which the Fourier amplitude spectra of far-field radiated body waves can be approximated as a truncated power law with frequency, and far-field body-wave displacements scale as the moment rate function together with constants that account for radiation pattern and geometric spreading. Assuming stress drop invariance for two different magnitude earthquakes, our derived scaling law can be used to estimate the corner frequencies of the two events, and then predict the spectral amplitudes of a larger event through application of a transfer function. 

     We assume that the duration of the radiated pulse is approximately the duration of the rupture, and that earthquake ruptures are approximately unilateral slip pulses (Heaton, 1990).

2019 Ridgecrest earthquake: observed M7.1 & simulated scenario M7.6 ground motions

Fig. 2. Spectral acceleration maps, southern California (Filippitzis et al., 2021). Left: M7.1 earthquake. Right: M6.4 earthquake. (a&b) T=1 s, (c&d) T=3 s, and (e&f) T=6 s. Damping ratio=5%. Circles: Community Seismic Network (Clayton et al., 2020) stations. Diamonds: SCSN & CSMIP stations. Black lines: maximum spectral acceleration directions. Note varying colorbar amplitude scales .

 

   To show an application of estimated ground motions using the method derived above for a scenario larger-magnitude earthquake, we compute ground motions for a scenario M7.6 Ridgecrest-like event using the M7.1 Ridgecrest observations. The synthetic ground motions are computed from available ground motions for the actual M7.1 mainshock event. The spectral scaling model is used to transform raw waveforms with respect to moment magnitude only, without varying the epicentral distance. All other parameters, such as epicenter and focal mechanism, are set constant.

     The Ridgecrest sequence (Fig. 1 shown in Abstract to the left) consisted of a M6.4 foreshock that occurred on July 4, 2019, the M7.1 mainshock on July 6, 2019, and several foreshocks and aftershocks with magnitude greater than 5. Here we use data from the two largest events of the sequence (M7.1 and M6.4), as well as from a M5.4 foreshock that occurred on July 5, 2019. Spectral accelerations computed from the Ridgecrest earthquake data (Fig. 2 above) show that

• The long period motion was amplified in the deeper parts of the LA basin by a factor of 5 relative to bedrock site values.

• For the longer periods, coherent patterns are present throughout the basin.

• For the shorter periods the motions are less spatially coherent, indicating a high level of scattering on the kilometer length scale

     We next apply the M7.6 simulated ground motions to dynamic analysis with finite element models of two instrumented buildings that represent common types of high-rise construction in Los Angeles and other seismically active urban cities in the U.S. We compute the commonly-used engineering parameter of maximum interstory drift in order to examine the geographical variations in its value for the largest of the observed long-period M7.1 Ridgecrest datasets, and for our simulated M7.6 accelerations. Specifically, we focus on the two sets of 20 seismic stations that produced the largest 3-s and 6-s PSA values to use as inputs into the dynamic analysis of the 15-story and 52-story high-rises, respectively. These 20 stations consist of SCSN, CSMIP, CSN and NP stations, located in south-central Los Angeles, West Los Angeles, the San Fernando Valley, and the San Gabriel Valley (Filippitzis et al., 2021). The goal is to investigate the spatial variations in maximum values of interstory drift, to compare the 3-s and 6-s results, and to evaluate the maximum values of the interstory drift for the scenario M7.6 event.

Application of simulated ground motions to two high-rises

Fig. 3. Finite-element model schematics of (a) 15-story and (b) 52-story buildings used in dynamic analysis.

 

     We apply the M7.6 simulated ground motions to dynamic analysis with finite element models of two instrumented buildings that represent common types of high-rise construction in Los Angeles and other seismically active urban cities in the U.S (Fig. 3). 

     For both buildings, we observe a significant increase in the maximum elastic interstory drift amplitude when comparing the M7.1 event to the scenario M7.6 scaled event across all stations. The peak interstory drift in the 15-story structure increases from 0.0032 (0.32%) on the 7th floor, to 0.0052 (0.52%) on the 13th floor (Fig. 4). For the 52-story, the peak interstory drift increases from 0.0045 (0.45%) on the 25th floor, to 0.0089 (0.89%) on the 29th floor (Fig. 5).

     The peak interstory drift values indicate geographic locations that may be most vulnerable to future large-magnitude, long-period earthquake ground motions for a scenario M7.6 occurring in the same source region. Specifically, the southern Los Angeles basin, central San Gabriel Valley sedimentary basin, and south-central San Fernando Valley sedimentary basin exhibit the largest values for the 3-s results. The western Los Angeles basin and south-central San Fernando Valley sedimentary basin exhibit the largest values for 6-s results.

     The results indicate that relative to the M7.1 earthquake, the M7.6 scenario earthquake would produce peak elastic interstory drifts that are 1.6 times larger for the 15-story and 2.0 times larger for the 52-story. For each building, the maximum levels of interstory drift associated with the scenario M7.6 event would likely lead to the onset of yielding (ASCE, 2017) and potential damage in the structures’ lateral system components.

     The scenario results are more pronounced in the 52-story high-rise, suggesting that the Ridgecrest event is relatively richer in low-frequency content such that the scaling procedure has a larger effect on amplifying low-frequency energy. Although the peak interstory drift calculations are computed for different sets of stations (the maximum 3-s PSAs for the 15-story vs. the maximum 6-s PSAs for the 52-story), these results indicate that the seismic hazard estimated by this method highlights the vulnerability of the long-period, tall structures.

Conclusions and Acknowledgments

     This method treats higher-frequency (≥1 Hz) energy based on the physics of the source mechanism and path effects. It does not require the inclusion of stochastic high-frequency wave energy, which would preclude accurate estimation of high-frequency amplification effects at the receiver site.

     In addition, the method can accommodate some path effects that have been observed at receiver sites, and that may not be incorporated into deterministic ground motion simulations. In the 2019 Ridgecrest earthquake, the azimuths associated with the largest horizontal direction of low-frequency ground motions in the Los Angeles basin (Fig. 2) (Filippitzis et al., 2021) are not explained by source rupture mechanism or directivity. Rather, they may be due to multipathing of seismic waves traveling into the northern Los Angeles basin from the adjacent Mojave basin to the north of the Transverse Ranges. As has been observed in previous studies, deterministic ground motion modeling underpredicts the maximum ground motions and, thus, maximum interstory drift values for the maximum 6-s PSA locations (Filippitzis et al., 2021; Kohler et al., 2021). Given a set of observations made from the same source rupture mechanism in the same source region, this method is straightforward to implement as a transfer function to observed ground motions in the far field.
